# Design Challenge: Chess Learning App for Kids & Teens
## Home Task | 3-4 hours | Mid-Senior Level

---

## Context

You're joining a team building **ChessKidAcademy**, a chess learning app for ages 6-18. The product is in beta with 5K users. Early chess board feedback shows:

- **Younger kids (6-8)** struggle with the piece movement rules and often make illegal moves
- **Pre-teens (9-12)** find the constant rule hints "baby-ish" and want a real challenge
- **Teens (13-18)** want competitive play, tactics training, and community features
- **Parents** want to see their kids learning, but don't want to force it
- **Retention is dropping** after week 2 across all age groups (different reasons for each)

Your task: **Design a solution that serves all three age groups (6-8, 9-12, 13-18) without building separate apps. One product, multiple needs, and mobile-friendly.**

---

## The Challenge

### Part 1: Problem Exploration (30-45 min)
Before jumping into design, analyze the situation:

1. **What's the root problem?** Is it interaction design, learning design, motivation, competitive needs, or something else?
2. **Who are we really designing for?** Just kids/teens, or also parents?
3. **What are we optimizing for?** Fun? Learning? Retention? Progression? Community? (Different age groups want different things)
4. **What constraints exist?** Budget, development time, team size (assume a small startup with 8 people)
5. **What data would help us decide?** What would you want to test or measure? (Remember: a 6-year-old's success metric differs from a 16-year-old's)

**Deliverable:** A 1-page problem statement or brief that shows your thinking. You can write this, sketch it, or use bullets. We care about *reasoning*, not polish.

---

### Part 2: Solution Design (90-120 min)
Design a solution addressing the core problem. Your solution should include:

#### A. The Core Interaction/Feature
**What's the single thing you're changing or adding?** (Examples: A settings system, a progression system, a new game mode, a visual design change, an onboarding flow)

- Write a 2-3 paragraph description of what users see and do
- Explain why this solves the problem(s) you identified
- Be specific about HOW kids interact (not just WHAT they see)

#### B. User Flows
Show how **at least three different users** experience your solution:

- **User A:** 7-year-old, first time playing, no chess knowledge, needs scaffolding
- **User B:** 11-year-old, basic chess knowledge, frustrated with "training wheels," wants a challenge
- **User C:** 15-year-old, solid player, wants tactics training and competitive ranking
- **Format:** Simple wireframes, user journey map, or written step-by-step (not pixel-perfect)

#### C. Key Design Decisions
For each major decision, explain:
- **What did you choose?** (e.g., "tap-to-select instead of drag-and-drop")
- **Why?** (reasoning: usability, accessibility, development complexity, learning science, etc.)
- **What are you NOT doing?** (trade-offs matter—show what you chose NOT to do and why)

Example:
> **Decision:** Tie "hint mode" to difficulty level, not age  
> **Why:** Avoids patronizing kids based on age; lets them self-select their learning style  
> **Trade-off:** Requires good onboarding so kids find the right mode. More complex than a hardcoded age rule.

#### D. Validation & Metrics
**How would you know if this works?**

- What would you measure? (e.g., time-to-first-win, session return rate, hint toggle-off rate)
- How would you test it? (e.g., A/B test, user interviews, playtesting with 5-6 kids)
- What's success? (e.g., "Both age groups return for week 3 at 50%+ rate")

---

### Part 3: Presentation (30 min)
Present your work as if you were pitching it to the team:

**Format options:**
1. **Written:** A clean 2-3 page document (PDF, Google Doc, Markdown)
2. **Slides:** 5-8 slides with visuals + speaker notes
3. **Video:** 5-minute walkthrough of your design + thinking
4. **Interactive prototype:** A clickable flow (Figma, Adobe XD, HTML/React)

**Structure:**
- Problem recap (1 min): What's broken, why it matters
- Solution (2 min): The core idea and how it works
- User evidence (1 min): How it serves both age groups
- Trade-offs (1 min): What you're betting on, what you're giving up
- Next steps (1 min): What would you test first?

**Tone:** Speak to designers, PMs, and engineers. Be clear and confident, but show your thinking, not just conclusions.

## Evaluation Criteria

We're looking for evidence of:

### 🎯 Problem-Solving (25%)
- Did you dig into the *root* problem, or just address symptoms?
- Did you identify what constraints/trade-offs exist?
- Did you consider multiple solutions before landing on one?
- Did you account for the fact that a 6-year-old, 12-year-old, and 16-year-old have fundamentally different needs?

---

### 👥 User Understanding (25%)
- Do you show deep thinking about *how different ages think* (not how adults think kids think)?
- Do you segment users meaningfully (by age, skill, motivation, identity)?
- Do you recognize that a 6-year-old, 12-year-old, and 16-year-old are fundamentally different users with different psychology?
- Do you consider edge cases (frustrated kids, social anxiety in teens, showing off to friends)?

---

### 🎨 Design Quality (20%)
- Is the solution elegant or overcomplicated?
- Does it avoid "solving" by adding more features/settings?
- Are wireframes/flows clear enough to follow? (Polish ≠ clarity)

---

### 📊 Validation Mindset (15%)
- Do you propose measurable success criteria?
- Do you show thinking about *how to test* before building?
- Do you identify what you're unsure about and want to validate?

---

### 💬 Communication (15%)
- Can you articulate your reasoning clearly?
- Do you show the *why* behind decisions?
- Is the presentation organized and easy to follow?

## FAQs

**Q: How polished should this be?**  
A: Fidelity doesn't matter. A hand-drawn wireframe is fine. We care about thinking, not pixels.

**Q: Can I use tools like Figma?**  
A: Yes, but no pressure. A Google Doc with text + crude sketches is equally valid.

**Q: What if I think the problem is different from what you described?**  
A: Great! Tell us what problem you see and why. We want to see your thinking, not your ability to follow instructions.

**Q: How long should this actually take?**  
A: 3-4 hours for most people. If you're significantly over, you're over-designing. If you're significantly under, you might be missing depth.

**Q: Can I ask us questions?**  
A: Yes! Email **Karam.ayman@thinktree.llc** if you need clarification. We may not answer everything (part of the challenge is making reasonable assumptions), but we're happy to help.

**Q: What if I want to change scope (e.g., design for a different age group)?**  
A: You can, but flag it clearly. Tell us why you think the constraint isn't useful and propose an alternative. This actually shows good thinking.

## What We're NOT Looking For

❌ Pixel-perfect mockups  
❌ Extensive market research  
❌ A fully fleshed-out product strategy (3-4 hours isn't enough)  
❌ A novel solution (thoughtful > clever)  
❌ Agreement on how we'd solve it  
❌ Video production quality (if you do video, content >> production)  
❌ A complete design system  
❌ Proof that this exact solution works (we're testing your process, not your clairvoyance)

---

## What We ARE Looking For

✅ Evidence of user research or empathy (interviews, assumptions you state explicitly)  
✅ Clear reasoning: "I chose X because..."  
✅ Trade-off thinking: "This costs us Y, but gains us Z"  
✅ Testability: How you'd validate before a full build  
✅ Humility: What you don't know and want to learn  
✅ Craft: Care taken in presentation, even if rough  
✅ Scope management: Solving the right problem at the right depth

## Final Note

This task is intentionally open-ended. There's no "right answer." We're interested in:

1. **How you think** — Do you dig or skim? Do you consider users or assumptions?
2. **How you make trade-offs** — What do you optimize for? What do you give up?
3. **How you communicate** — Can you explain your reasoning to non-designers?
4. **How do you validate** — Do you test assumptions or rely on intuition?

This is the core of mid-senior design work. The rest is tools and taste.
